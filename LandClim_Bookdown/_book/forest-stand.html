We describe stand level processes and properties

4.1 Forest stand scale processes

LandClim distinguishes between three different types of stand-scale processes:

  1. Tree growth,

  2. Tree mortality

  3. Tree regeneration.

These processes are simulated for each cohort, i.e. groups of trees of the same species and the same age within each patch (i.e. landscape grid-cell). 

LandClim calculates the average biomass of an individual tree (\(B_i\)) and the number of trees (\(N_c\)) within each cohort. The biomass (\(B_{c}\)) per cohort is thus calculated as:

\[ \begin{equation} B_{c} = B_{i} \cdot N_{c} \tag{4.1} \end{equation} \]

The change of cohort biomass per year is calculated with the differential equation (4.2). The first term tracks the changes in biomass of individual plants (i.e. representation of growth), whereas the second term tracks changes in the number of individuals (\(N_{c}\)) within a cohort (thus accounting for mortality).

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{dB_{c}}{dt} = \frac{dB_{i}}{dt} \cdot N_{c} + \frac{dN_{c}}{dt} %\cdot B_{i} \tag{4.2} \end{equation} \]

4.1.1 Treegrowth

In LandClim, tree growth is simulated via functional relationships, which are based on a species-specific maximum growth rate (\(r_{s}\); growth under optimum environmental conditions) and a species-specific maximum biomass (\(K_{s}\); maximum biomass an individual tree of a certain species can reach), representing growth under optimum environmental conditions. To account for the reduction of growth under non-optimum environmental conditions, reduction factors by light-, drought- and temperature-limitation are considered in the growth formulation. 

4.1.1.1 Tree growth formulation

Under non-optimal environmental conditions, a number of growth-limiting factors reduce the optimal growth rate. These factors include, (1) light limitation (light_rf), (2) degree-day limitation (DD_rf), and (3) drought limitation (drStr_rf). Values for the reduction factor range between 0 (maximum limitation) and 1 (no limitation).

These factors are implemented via Liebig’s ‘Law of the Minimum.’ Thus, the realized growth rate (\(r_{i}(t)\)) is calculated as the optimum growth rate (\(r_{s}\)) reduced by the most limiting environmental factor (Eq. (4.3)).

\[ \begin{equation} r_{i}(t) = r_{s} \cdot min(light\_rf(t) , DD_rf(t), drStr_rf(t)) \tag{4.3} \end{equation} \]

The realized maximum biomass \(K_i\)(t) is also limited by environmental conditions, which reduce the maximum biomass (\(K_s\)) by the minimum of growing degree-days (DD_rf) or drought (drStr_rf).


\[ \begin{equation} K_{i}(t) = K_{s} \cdot min(DD_rf(t), drStr_rf(t)) \tag{4.4} \end{equation} \]

Three different phenomenological growth models can be selected to simulate tree growth in LandClim: (1) Logistic, (2) Gompertz and (3) von Bertalanffy. For further descriptions about the background of each equation, and for a comparison between the different models, see \citet{Elkin2013}. Equations used for the different growth models are:

I believe these equations are prediciting the individual tree biomass $B_i$, that multiplied by $N_c$ would give $B_c$"

Type ‘LOGISTIC’

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{dB_{c}}{dt} =  r_{i}(t) \cdot (1.0 - \frac{B\_{i}(t)}{K_{i}(t)} \cdot B_{i}(t)  \end{equation} \]

Type ‘GOMPERTZ’

\begin{equation}

\\frac{dB\_{c}}{dt} =  r\_{i}(t) \\cdot ( log(K\_{i}) - log( B\_{i}(t))) \\cdot B\_{i}(t)  

\end{equation}


Type ‘BERTALANFFY’

\begin{equation}

\\frac{dB\_{c}}{dt} =  3.0 \\cdot r\_{i}(t) \\cdot K\_{i}(t)\^{1/3} \\cdot B\_{i}(t)\^{2/3} - B\_{i}(t) 

\end{equation}


By default, the LOGISTIC growth model is used.
